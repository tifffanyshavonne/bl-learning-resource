What the research actually says
Common myths that cost students time
What feels like studying
  • Re-reading notes and textbooks
  • Highlighting everything important
  • Studying one subject for a long block
  • Matching instruction to "learning styles"
  • Cramming the night before a test
What actually builds memory
  • Self-testing from memory (blank recall, flashcards)
  • Explaining concepts in your own words
  • Spreading study across multiple sessions
  • Mixing topics within a study session
  • Reviewing material well before the test
Key finding: Students who re-read feel more confident going into tests — but those who practice retrieving information from memory consistently outperform them. The discomfort of testing yourself is a signal that learning is happening.
Cognitive foundations
Three things every BL teacher should know about how boys learn
Attention is limited & selective
Boys can only focus on a few things at once. Reducing extraneous noise — cluttered slides, competing audio and text — directly frees up mental bandwidth for learning. Less is more in instructional design.
Working memory has a ceiling
Active thinking happens in working memory, which holds only a small amount at a time. Chunking material and connecting new content to prior knowledge lowers the load and improves retention.
Memory is reconstructive
Students don't "record" information like a camera — each recall is a reconstruction. This is why practice retrieval, not re-exposure, is what strengthens memory over time.
The six evidence-backed strategies
What to prioritize — and how it looks at BL
★ Highest impact
Retrieval practice
Having students pull information from memory — rather than re-reading — is the most evidence-backed strategy for durable learning. Low-stakes quizzes, exit tickets, and blank-page recall all qualify.
At BL Middle School Start each class with a 3-question warm-up on yesterday's material. No grades — just practice. In Language Lab, replace review reading with write-what-you-remember exercises.
★ Highest impact
Spaced practice
Reviewing material across multiple sessions rather than one long block dramatically improves long-term retention. The "spacing effect" is one of the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology.
At BL Middle School Build spaced review into unit planning — return to Week 1 content in Week 3. Math teachers already sequence this well; English and History have room to formalize it.
Elaboration
Asking "why does this work?" and "how does it connect to what you know?" builds richer mental models. "Explain it in your own words" is one of the most powerful teacher moves.
At BL Middle School History and English are natural homes. Prompt: "Write 3 sentences connecting today's reading to something from your own life or something we covered before."
Interleaving
Mixing different problem types or topics within a session builds the ability to identify which strategy to use — a key skill for tests and real-world application.
At BL Middle School Math is the most natural fit. Instead of 20 identical problems, assign mixed problem sets drawing on prior units. Feels harder — and works better.
Concrete examples
Abstract concepts stick when anchored with multiple specific, varied examples — especially those tied to students' real experience. Varied examples enable genuine generalization.
At BL Middle School Science teachers already do this through lab-based work. Ask students to generate their own examples — this combines elaboration and concrete examples in one move.
Dual coding
Combining verbal explanations with complementary visuals that encode the same information spatially improves retention. Student-generated drawings are especially powerful.
At BL Middle School STEAM and Innovation Program naturally support this. Ask students in any subject to sketch a concept before writing — this activates a second memory pathway.
Practical next steps
What staff and students can do right now
Staff
Add a 5-minute retrieval warm-up to the start of every class — no grading, just practice. Three questions from last week's material. Students write from memory before anything else is shown.
Staff
Audit your unit plans for spacing. When do students see Week 1 content again? If the answer is "only on the final," there's a spaced practice gap worth addressing.
Staff
Replace re-reading homework with retrieval homework. Instead of "re-read pages 40–55," assign "write everything you remember from pages 40–55 without looking." Same content, far better outcome.
Students
Teach the closed-book recall method. After reading any section, close the book and write what you remember. Check after. This feels harder than highlighting — that's the point. That discomfort is learning.
Students
Reframe how boys understand "hard." When practice tests feel difficult, that is the strategy working — not a sign they don't know the material. This mindset shift is huge for middle schoolers.
Both
Post the six strategy posters (free from learningscientists.org) in classrooms and advisor spaces. When teachers and students share the same vocabulary for study strategies, the whole culture shifts.
Both
Address the "learning styles" myth directly. Many BL boys have been told they are visual or verbal learners. The research shows no evidence this matters — and acting on it can actually narrow how boys approach material.
